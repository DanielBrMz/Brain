# Abstract Algebra

> *"Algebra is the offer made by the devil to the mathematician. The devil says: 'I will give you this powerful machine, it will answer any question you like. All you need to do is give me your soul: give up geometry and you will have this marvelous machine.'"* — Michael Atiyah

## Motivation

Abstract algebra studies algebraic structures — groups, rings, fields, modules — by identifying their essential axioms and deriving consequences. This abstraction reveals deep connections: the integers and polynomials share the same algebraic structure (both are PIDs); symmetries of geometric objects form groups; the impossibility of trisecting angles or solving quintics by radicals are theorems of field theory. Abstract algebra is the language of modern number theory, algebraic geometry, cryptography, and theoretical physics.

## Prerequisites

- [[linear-algebra]] (vector spaces as a key example of algebraic structure; familiarity with abstract proof)
- Modular arithmetic and basic number theory (gcd, primes, Euclidean algorithm)
- Mathematical maturity

## Part I: Group Theory (Weeks 1--5)

### Week 1: Groups and Subgroups

**Topics:**
- Definition of a group: set G with binary operation satisfying closure, associativity, identity, inverses
- Examples: Z under addition, Z/nZ, S_n (symmetric group), GL_n(F), dihedral groups D_n, cyclic groups
- Subgroups; subgroup criterion (nonempty, closed under operation and inverses)
- Cyclic groups: generated by a single element; every subgroup of a cyclic group is cyclic
- Order of an element; the group generated by an element

**Key Observation:** Groups capture the notion of symmetry. The symmetric group S_n is the group of all symmetries (permutations) of {1,...,n}; the dihedral group D_n is the symmetry group of a regular n-gon. Cayley's theorem (Week 3) says every group is a group of symmetries.

### Week 2: Quotient Groups and Homomorphisms

**Topics:**
- Cosets: aH = {ah : h ∈ H}; left vs right cosets
- Lagrange's theorem: |H| divides |G| for finite groups; consequence: |a| divides |G|
- Normal subgroups: N ◁ G iff gNg^{-1} = N for all g
- Quotient groups G/N (well-defined operation iff N is normal)
- Group homomorphisms; kernel and image
- The isomorphism theorems

**Key Results:**
- **Lagrange's Theorem:** If H ≤ G with G finite, then |H| divides |G|, and [G:H] = |G|/|H|.
  - *Proof:* The left cosets of H partition G into sets of equal size |H|.
  - *Corollary:* The order of any element divides |G|. Groups of prime order are cyclic.
- **First Isomorphism Theorem:** If φ: G → H is a homomorphism, then G/ker(φ) ≅ im(φ).
  - *Proof sketch:* Define ψ: G/ker(φ) → im(φ) by ψ(g·ker φ) = φ(g). Check well-defined (uses normality of kernel), injective (ker ψ is trivial), surjective (by definition of image).
  - *Importance:* This is arguably the most used theorem in all of algebra. It connects quotients and images.

### Week 3: Group Actions and Sylow Theorems

**Topics:**
- Group actions: G acts on set X; orbits and stabilizers
- Orbit-stabilizer theorem: |G| = |Orb(x)| · |Stab(x)|
- Cayley's theorem: every group embeds in a symmetric group
- Class equation: |G| = |Z(G)| + Σ [G : C_G(g_i)] (sum over conjugacy class representatives)
- p-groups: groups of order p^n; center of a p-group is nontrivial
- Sylow theorems

**Key Results:**
- **Sylow Theorems:** Let |G| = p^a · m with gcd(p, m) = 1.
  1. G has a subgroup of order p^a (a Sylow p-subgroup).
  2. All Sylow p-subgroups are conjugate.
  3. The number n_p of Sylow p-subgroups satisfies n_p ≡ 1 (mod p) and n_p | m.
  - *Proof sketch (Sylow I):* By induction on |G|. If p divides |Z(G)|, Cauchy's theorem gives an element of order p in the center; quotient out and apply induction. If p does not divide |Z(G)|, the class equation shows some conjugacy class has size not divisible by p; the centralizer of a representative has order divisible by p^a, and induction applies.
  - *Applications:* The Sylow theorems are the primary tool for classifying groups of small order. Example: every group of order 15 is cyclic (n_3 | 5, n_3 ≡ 1 mod 3 ⟹ n_3 = 1; similarly n_5 = 1; unique Sylow subgroups are normal; G ≅ Z/3Z × Z/5Z ≅ Z/15Z).

### Week 4: Structure of Finite Groups

**Topics:**
- Direct and semidirect products
- Classification of finite abelian groups: every finite abelian group is a direct sum of cyclic groups of prime power order (equivalently, Z/n_1Z × ... × Z/n_kZ with n_i | n_{i+1})
  - *This is a special case of the structure theorem for finitely generated modules over a PID.*
- Simple groups; composition series; Jordan-Holder theorem
- Solvable groups; A_n is simple for n ≥ 5 (and hence S_n is not solvable for n ≥ 5)
  - *This is the algebraic reason the quintic is not solvable by radicals.*
- Nilpotent groups; relationship to p-groups

**Key Results:**
- **Fundamental Theorem of Finite Abelian Groups:** Every finite abelian group is isomorphic to Z/p_1^{a_1}Z × ... × Z/p_k^{a_k}Z for primes p_i and positive integers a_i, unique up to reordering.
- **Jordan-Holder Theorem:** Any two composition series of a finite group have the same length and the same composition factors (up to permutation and isomorphism).

### Week 5: Free Groups, Presentations, and Further Topics

**Topics:**
- Free groups and universal property
- Group presentations: generators and relations
- Free products and amalgamated products
- Burnside's lemma (counting orbits): |X/G| = (1/|G|) Σ_{g∈G} |Fix(g)|
- Brief introduction to representation theory: group homomorphisms G → GL(V) (connection to [[linear-algebra]])

## Part II: Ring Theory (Weeks 6--9)

### Week 6: Rings and Ideals

**Topics:**
- Definition of a ring (with unity); commutative rings
- Examples: Z, Z/nZ, polynomial rings F[x], matrix rings M_n(F), function rings
- Subrings; ideals (left, right, two-sided)
- Quotient rings R/I; ring homomorphisms
- Isomorphism theorems for rings (analogous to groups)

### Week 7: Integral Domains and Factorization

**Topics:**
- Integral domains: commutative rings with no zero divisors
- Units, associates, irreducibles, primes
- In general: prime ⟹ irreducible; converse holds in UFDs but not always
- Principal ideal domains (PIDs): every ideal is (a) for some a
- Unique factorization domains (UFDs): existence and uniqueness of factorization into irreducibles
- PID ⟹ UFD (but not conversely: Z[x] is a UFD but not a PID)
- Euclidean domains ⟹ PIDs ⟹ UFDs

**Key Results:**
- **Every PID is a UFD.**
  - *Proof sketch:* Existence of factorization: if a is not irreducible, write a = bc (neither a unit). If the process doesn't terminate, we get an ascending chain of ideals (a) ⊂ (b) ⊂ ..., contradicting the ascending chain condition in PIDs (proved via: in a PID, the union of an ascending chain of ideals is principal). Uniqueness: in a PID, irreducible ⟹ prime (proved via: if p | ab and p is irreducible, then (p, a) = (1) or (p), leading to p | a or p | b).

### Week 8: Polynomial Rings

**Topics:**
- F[x] is a Euclidean domain (hence PID, hence UFD) when F is a field
- Division algorithm, gcd, irreducibility
- Eisenstein's criterion for irreducibility over Z (and Q via Gauss's lemma)
- Gauss's lemma: if f ∈ Z[x] is reducible over Q, it is reducible over Z
- R[x] is a UFD whenever R is (Gauss's theorem)
- Polynomial rings in several variables: F[x_1,...,x_n] is a UFD but not a PID for n ≥ 2
- Hilbert's basis theorem: if R is Noetherian, so is R[x]

**Key Result:**
- **Hilbert's Basis Theorem:** If R is Noetherian (every ideal is finitely generated), then R[x] is Noetherian.
  - *Proof sketch:* Given an ideal I ⊂ R[x], consider the ideal of leading coefficients in R. By Noetherian property, it is finitely generated. The corresponding polynomials, plus generators for lower-degree parts, generate I.

### Week 9: Localization, PIDs, and Modules

**Topics:**
- Localization of rings: S^{-1}R (e.g., field of fractions)
- Modules over a ring (generalization of vector spaces over a field)
- Free modules, submodules, quotient modules
- Structure theorem for finitely generated modules over a PID: M ≅ R^r ⊕ R/(a_1) ⊕ ... ⊕ R/(a_k)
  - *This unifies:* classification of finite abelian groups (R = Z) and Jordan/rational canonical forms (R = F[x], module = vector space with an endomorphism)
- Noetherian modules; exact sequences

## Part III: Field Theory and Galois Theory (Weeks 10--15)

### Week 10: Field Extensions

**Topics:**
- Field extensions F ⊆ E (equivalently, E/F); degree [E:F] = dim_F(E)
- Algebraic vs transcendental elements; minimal polynomial
- Simple extensions F(α); F(α) ≅ F[x]/(min_α(x)) when α is algebraic
- Tower law: [E:F] = [E:K][K:F]
- Algebraic extensions; algebraic closure
- Finite fields: F_{p^n} exists and is unique (up to isomorphism); multiplicative group is cyclic

### Week 11: Splitting Fields and Normality

**Topics:**
- Splitting field of a polynomial: smallest extension in which f factors completely into linears
- Existence and uniqueness (up to isomorphism) of splitting fields
- Normal extensions: every irreducible polynomial with one root in E splits completely in E
- Separable extensions: minimal polynomials have no repeated roots
- Perfect fields (every extension is separable); all fields of characteristic 0 and all finite fields are perfect
- Primitive element theorem: every finite separable extension is simple

### Week 12: Galois Theory — Foundations

**Topics:**
- Automorphisms of field extensions; the Galois group Gal(E/F) = Aut_F(E)
- Galois extensions: normal and separable; equivalently |Gal(E/F)| = [E:F]
- Fixed fields: E^H = {α ∈ E : σ(α) = α for all σ ∈ H}
- The Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory

**Key Result:**
- **Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory:** Let E/F be a finite Galois extension with Galois group G = Gal(E/F). There is an inclusion-reversing bijection:
  - {subgroups H of G} ↔ {intermediate fields F ⊆ K ⊆ E}
  - H ↦ E^H (fixed field)
  - K ↦ Gal(E/K)
  - Moreover: [E:E^H] = |H|, [E^H:F] = [G:H], and H is normal in G iff E^H/F is Galois, in which case Gal(E^H/F) ≅ G/H.
  - *Proof sketch:* Key steps: (1) |Gal(E/F)| = [E:F] for Galois extensions. (2) E^{Gal(E/F)} = F (Artin's theorem: if G is a finite group of automorphisms, [E:E^G] = |G|). (3) The maps are mutually inverse by combining these facts.
  - *Importance:* This converts field-theoretic questions (which are hard) into group-theoretic questions (which have powerful tools — Sylow, classification of abelian groups, etc.).

### Week 13: Applications of Galois Theory

**Topics:**
- Galois groups of polynomials; discriminant; relationship to S_n, A_n
- Cyclotomic fields and cyclotomic polynomials; Gal(Q(ζ_n)/Q) ≅ (Z/nZ)^×
- Solvability by radicals: a polynomial is solvable by radicals iff its Galois group is a solvable group
- **Abel-Ruffini theorem:** The general polynomial of degree ≥ 5 is not solvable by radicals (because S_n is not solvable for n ≥ 5)
- Classical straightedge-and-compass constructions: an element is constructible iff its minimal polynomial has degree a power of 2
  - Impossibility of doubling the cube (∛2 has degree 3)
  - Impossibility of trisecting a general angle (cos 20° has degree 3)
  - Impossibility of squaring the circle (π is transcendental — Lindemann)
  - Constructibility of regular n-gons: iff n = 2^k · p_1 · ... · p_m where the p_i are distinct Fermat primes

### Week 14: Finite Fields and Further Topics

**Topics:**
- Detailed structure of finite fields: existence, uniqueness, subfield lattice
- Frobenius endomorphism: x ↦ x^p; generates Gal(F_{p^n}/F_p) ≅ Z/nZ
- Wedderburn's little theorem: every finite division ring is a field
- Transcendence degree; transcendence bases
- Introduction to algebraic geometry: varieties as zero sets of polynomials, Hilbert's Nullstellensatz

**Key Result:**
- **Wedderburn's Little Theorem:** Every finite division ring is commutative (hence a field).
  - *Proof sketch:* Use the class equation for the multiplicative group, combined with cyclotomic polynomial divisibility arguments, to show the center must equal the entire ring.

### Week 15: Category-Theoretic Perspective

**Topics:**
- Categories, functors, natural transformations (briefly)
- Groups, rings, fields, modules as categories
- Universal properties: free groups, tensor products, localization, polynomial rings
- Exactness and diagram chasing
- The unifying language: many constructions in algebra are instances of universal properties

## Applications

### Cryptography
- **RSA:** Based on the difficulty of factoring in Z; uses Euler's theorem (a^{φ(n)} ≡ 1 mod n)
- **Elliptic Curve Cryptography:** Group law on elliptic curves over finite fields
- **Diffie-Hellman:** Relies on the discrete logarithm problem in cyclic groups
- **AES:** Operates in the finite field F_{2^8} = F_2[x]/(x^8+x^4+x^3+x+1)
- **Post-quantum cryptography:** Lattice-based schemes use module theory over polynomial rings

### Coding Theory
- **Linear codes** are subspaces of F_q^n (connection to [[linear-algebra]])
- **Cyclic codes** correspond to ideals in F_q[x]/(x^n - 1); BCH and Reed-Solomon codes
- **Algebraic geometry codes** use rational points on curves over finite fields

### Physics
- **Symmetry groups** in physics: SO(3) for rotations, SU(2) for spin, Lorentz group for special relativity
- **Gauge theories:** structure group of a principal bundle (U(1) for electromagnetism, SU(3) for QCD)
- **Representation theory** of Lie groups governs particle classifications (the Eightfold Way)
- **Galois connections** appear in quantum logic and lattice theory
