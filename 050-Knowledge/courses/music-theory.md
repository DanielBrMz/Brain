# Music Theory

> [[../literature-syllabus|Literature & Language Syllabus]] | Related: [[linguistics]], [[literary-theory]]

## Motivation

Music theory is the study of the practices and possibilities of music — the grammar behind the art. Just as understanding syntax deepens your appreciation of literature, understanding harmony, counterpoint, and form reveals the architecture beneath what you hear. This course covers Western tonal music from fundamentals through advanced harmony and form, with attention to jazz harmony and Latin American musical traditions. Whether you compose, perform, produce, or simply listen, theory transforms your relationship with music from passive reception to active understanding.

## Prerequisites

- No formal prerequisites
- Ability to read basic musical notation is helpful but can be developed alongside the course
- An instrument or access to a piano/keyboard is strongly recommended

## I. Fundamentals: Pitch and Rhythm

### Pitch

- **Notes:** C D E F G A B (repeating in octaves)
- **Octave:** Frequency ratio 2:1; same pitch class, different register
- **Semitone (half step):** Smallest interval in Western music (e.g., E-F, B-C, or any note to its sharp/flat)
- **Whole tone (whole step):** Two semitones
- **Enharmonic equivalence:** C# = Db, F# = Gb (same pitch, different name/function)
- **Accidentals:** Sharp (#), flat (b), natural, double sharp (x), double flat (bb)

### The Chromatic Scale

All 12 semitones within an octave: C C# D D# E F F# G G# A A# B

### Rhythm

- **Beat:** Regular pulse
- **Meter:** Grouping of beats — **simple** (subdivisions in 2) vs. **compound** (subdivisions in 3)
  - Simple: 2/4, 3/4, 4/4 (common time)
  - Compound: 6/8, 9/8, 12/8
  - Asymmetric/irregular: 5/4, 7/8, 11/8
- **Tempo:** Speed of the beat (BPM)
- **Syncopation:** Emphasis on weak beats or off-beats
- **Polyrhythm:** Simultaneous contrasting rhythmic patterns (e.g., 3 against 2)
- **Swing:** Unequal subdivision of the beat (jazz, blues — long-short instead of even)

## II. Scales and Modes

### Major Scale

Whole-whole-half-whole-whole-whole-half (W W H W W W H)

C major: C D E F G A B C

### Natural Minor Scale

W H W W H W W

A minor: A B C D E F G A (relative minor of C major — same notes)

### Other Minor Scales

- **Harmonic minor:** Raised 7th degree → W H W W H (W+H) H
  - A harmonic minor: A B C D E F G# A
  - Creates the leading tone; augmented 2nd between 6th and 7th degrees

- **Melodic minor:** Ascending: raised 6th and 7th; descending: natural minor
  - A melodic minor ascending: A B C D E F# G# A
  - Jazz melodic minor: raised 6th and 7th both ascending and descending

### Church Modes

Built on successive degrees of the major scale:

| Mode | Pattern (relative to major) | Character |
|------|----------------------------|-----------|
| **Ionian** (I) | 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 | Major — bright |
| **Dorian** (ii) | 1 2 b3 4 5 6 b7 | Minor with major 6th — jazzy, bittersweet |
| **Phrygian** (iii) | 1 b2 b3 4 5 b6 b7 | Dark, Spanish/flamenco flavor |
| **Lydian** (IV) | 1 2 3 #4 5 6 7 | Major with raised 4th — dreamy, floating |
| **Mixolydian** (V) | 1 2 3 4 5 6 b7 | Major with flat 7th — bluesy, rock |
| **Aeolian** (vi) | 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 b7 | Natural minor |
| **Locrian** (vii) | 1 b2 b3 4 b5 b6 b7 | Diminished — unstable, rarely used as tonic |

### Pentatonic Scales

- **Major pentatonic:** 1 2 3 5 6 (remove 4th and 7th — no semitones)
- **Minor pentatonic:** 1 b3 4 5 b7
- Universal across cultures; foundational in blues, rock, folk, East Asian music

### Blues Scale

Minor pentatonic + b5 (the "blue note"): 1 b3 4 b5 5 b7

### Other Scales

- **Whole tone:** All whole steps — augmented, impressionistic (Debussy)
- **Diminished (octatonic):** Alternating W-H or H-W — symmetric, jazz/20th century
- **Bebop scales:** Add chromatic passing tone to maintain chord tones on strong beats
- **Altered scale:** 7th mode of melodic minor — used over altered dominant chords

## III. Intervals

### Classification

An interval = distance between two pitches.

| Semitones | Name | Example |
|-----------|------|---------|
| 0 | Perfect unison (P1) | C-C |
| 1 | Minor 2nd (m2) | C-Db |
| 2 | Major 2nd (M2) | C-D |
| 3 | Minor 3rd (m3) | C-Eb |
| 4 | Major 3rd (M3) | C-E |
| 5 | Perfect 4th (P4) | C-F |
| 6 | Tritone (A4/d5) | C-F#/Gb |
| 7 | Perfect 5th (P5) | C-G |
| 8 | Minor 6th (m6) | C-Ab |
| 9 | Major 6th (M6) | C-A |
| 10 | Minor 7th (m7) | C-Bb |
| 11 | Major 7th (M7) | C-B |
| 12 | Perfect octave (P8) | C-C |

### Interval Quality

- **Perfect:** Unison, 4th, 5th, octave
- **Major/minor:** 2nd, 3rd, 6th, 7th
- **Augmented:** Half step larger than perfect or major
- **Diminished:** Half step smaller than perfect or minor
- **Inversion:** M↔m, A↔d, P↔P; interval + inversion = 9 (e.g., M3 + m6 = 9)

### Consonance and Dissonance

- **Perfect consonances:** P1, P5, P8
- **Imperfect consonances:** M3, m3, M6, m6
- **Mild dissonances:** M2, m7
- **Sharp dissonances:** m2, M7, tritone (A4/d5)

## IV. Chords

### Triads

Three notes stacked in thirds:

| Type | Formula | Example (on C) | Sound |
|------|---------|----------------|-------|
| Major | 1 3 5 | C E G | Bright, stable |
| Minor | 1 b3 5 | C Eb G | Dark, stable |
| Diminished | 1 b3 b5 | C Eb Gb | Tense, unstable |
| Augmented | 1 3 #5 | C E G# | Mysterious, unresolved |

### Seventh Chords

Add a 7th above the root:

| Type | Formula | Symbol | Example |
|------|---------|--------|---------|
| Major 7th | 1 3 5 7 | Cmaj7 | C E G B |
| Dominant 7th | 1 3 5 b7 | C7 | C E G Bb |
| Minor 7th | 1 b3 5 b7 | Cm7 | C Eb G Bb |
| Half-diminished | 1 b3 b5 b7 | Cm7b5 / Cø7 | C Eb Gb Bb |
| Fully diminished | 1 b3 b5 bb7 | Cdim7 / C°7 | C Eb Gb Bbb(=A) |
| Minor-major 7th | 1 b3 5 7 | Cm(maj7) | C Eb G B |

### Extensions

- **9th:** Add the 9th (= 2nd up an octave)
- **11th:** Add the 11th (= 4th up an octave)
- **13th:** Add the 13th (= 6th up an octave)
- **Altered extensions:** b9, #9, #11, b13 — used on dominant chords

### Inversions

| Position | Bass note | Figured bass |
|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Root position | Root | 5/3 |
| 1st inversion | 3rd | 6/3 |
| 2nd inversion | 5th | 6/4 |
| 3rd inversion (7th chords) | 7th | 4/2 |

## V. Diatonic Harmony and Chord Progressions

### Diatonic Chords in Major

| Degree | Roman numeral | Triad | Seventh |
|--------|--------------|-------|---------|
| I | I | Major | Maj7 |
| ii | ii | Minor | m7 |
| iii | iii | Minor | m7 |
| IV | IV | Major | Maj7 |
| V | V | Major | Dom7 |
| vi | vi | Minor | m7 |
| vii° | vii° | Diminished | m7b5 |

### Functional Harmony

- **Tonic (T):** I, vi (stability, resolution)
- **Predominant (PD):** ii, IV (approach dominant)
- **Dominant (D):** V, vii° (tension, wants to resolve to I)
- **Basic progression:** T → PD → D → T

### Common Progressions

- **I - IV - V - I:** The foundation of Western tonal music
- **I - V - vi - IV:** The "pop" progression (ubiquitous in popular music)
- **ii - V - I:** The backbone of jazz harmony
- **I - vi - IV - V:** 1950s doo-wop ("Heart and Soul")
- **12-bar blues:** I I I I / IV IV I I / V IV I V (with dominant 7ths throughout)
- **Circle of fifths progression:** vi - ii - V - I (iii - vi - ii - V - I extended)
- **Pachelbel Canon:** I - V - vi - iii - IV - I - IV - V

### Secondary Dominants

A dominant chord (or vii°) temporarily tonicizing a diatonic chord:
- **V/V:** Dominant of the dominant (e.g., D7 → G in the key of C)
- **V/ii, V/iii, V/IV, V/vi:** Each briefly makes its target sound like tonic
- Creates chromatic color without leaving the key

### Modulation

- **Pivot chord (common chord) modulation:** Chord functions in both old and new key
- **Direct (phrase) modulation:** New key simply starts at a phrase boundary
- **Chromatic modulation:** Use chromatic alteration to reach new key
- **Common modulations:** To dominant (V), relative minor (vi), relative major (III), subdominant (IV)

## VI. Voice Leading and Counterpoint

### Voice Leading Principles

- **Smooth voice leading:** Move each voice by the smallest interval possible
- **Contrary motion:** Voices move in opposite directions (preferred)
- **Avoid parallel 5ths and octaves:** Destroys voice independence (fundamental rule since Renaissance)
- **Resolve tendency tones:** Leading tone → tonic; 7th of V7 → 3rd of I
- **Common tone retention:** Keep shared tones in the same voice

### Species Counterpoint (Fux, 1725)

Systematic method for learning to write independent melodic lines:

1. **First species:** Note against note; only consonances; contrary/oblique motion
2. **Second species:** Two notes against one; passing tones on weak beats
3. **Third species:** Four notes against one; more passing and neighbor tones
4. **Fourth species:** Syncopated — suspensions (dissonance on strong beat resolves stepwise down)
5. **Fifth species (florid):** Combination of all species — free counterpoint

### Cadences

- **Authentic cadence (PAC):** V → I, soprano on tonic, root position — strongest closure
- **Imperfect authentic (IAC):** V → I with weaker voice leading (soprano not on tonic, or inversion)
- **Half cadence (HC):** Phrase ends on V — suspense, incompleteness
- **Plagal cadence:** IV → I ("Amen" cadence)
- **Deceptive cadence:** V → vi (expected I, got vi — surprise continuation)

### Harmonic Analysis

- Roman numeral analysis: identify chords by function in key
- Figured bass realization: realize chords from bass line + figures
- Non-chord tones: passing (P), neighbor (N), suspension (S), anticipation (A), appoggiatura (App), escape tone (E), pedal point

## VII. Musical Form

### Phrase Structure

- **Period:** Two phrases (antecedent → half cadence; consequent → authentic cadence)
- **Sentence:** Presentation (basic idea + repetition) → continuation → cadence
- **Phrase group:** Phrases that relate but don't form a period

### Binary Form (AB)

- Two sections, each usually repeated (||: A :||: B :||)
- **Rounded binary:** B section returns to opening material (A B A')
- Common in Baroque dance movements

### Ternary Form (ABA)

- Three sections; A returns after contrasting B (often with da capo)
- Minuet and Trio; da capo aria

### Sonata Form

The most important form in Classical music:

1. **Exposition:** Theme 1 (tonic) → transition → Theme 2 (dominant/relative major) → closing
2. **Development:** Themes fragmented, modulated, combined; harmonic instability
3. **Recapitulation:** Theme 1 (tonic) → transition → Theme 2 (now in tonic) → closing/coda

### Rondo Form

A recurring theme alternates with contrasting episodes:
- **ABACA** (5-part rondo)
- **ABACABA** (7-part rondo)

### Theme and Variations

Statement of theme followed by successive variations:
- Change melody, harmony, rhythm, texture, tempo, mode, key
- Each variation recognizably related to the theme

### Fugue

- Not a form per se but a procedure/texture
- **Exposition:** Subject enters in each voice successively (tonic → dominant answer → tonic → etc.)
- **Episodes:** Sequential passages connecting subject entries
- **Devices:** Stretto (overlapping entries), inversion, augmentation, diminution, retrograde

### 12-Bar Blues Form

| Bars 1-4 | Bars 5-8 | Bars 9-12 |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| I7 I7 I7 I7 | IV7 IV7 I7 I7 | V7 IV7 I7 V7 |

Variants: quick change (IV in bar 2), jazz blues (with ii-V substitutions), minor blues.

### 32-Bar AABA (Standard Song Form)

- A (8 bars) — A (8 bars) — B (8 bars, "bridge") — A (8 bars)
- Dominant form in Tin Pan Alley and Great American Songbook
- Examples: "Over the Rainbow," "Body and Soul," "Take the A Train"

## VIII. Composition and Arranging

### Melody Writing

- **Conjunct motion** (stepwise) with occasional leaps; leaps often filled in
- **Contour:** Shape of the melody — arch, ascending, descending, wave
- **Climax:** Usually one high point per phrase
- **Rhythm:** Variety; avoid monotony; syncopation adds interest
- **Motivic development:** Repetition, sequence, inversion, augmentation, fragmentation

### Harmonization

- Choose chords that support the melody notes as chord tones
- Multiple harmonizations possible for any melody — voice leading guides the choice
- Reharmonization: substitute chords for new color (especially in jazz)

### Arranging

- Distribute melody, harmony, bass, and rhythm across instruments
- **Voicing:** How notes of a chord are distributed (close vs. open position, drop voicings)
- **Texture:** Monophony, homophony, polyphony, heterophony
- **Orchestration:** Timbral choices; instrument ranges, combinations, doublings

## IX. Music History Overview

### Baroque (c. 1600-1750)

- **Characteristics:** Basso continuo, terraced dynamics, ornamentation, affections
- **Forms:** Fugue, suite, concerto grosso, opera, cantata, oratorio
- **Key figures:** Bach, Handel, Vivaldi, Monteverdi, Corelli
- Tonal harmony solidifies; major/minor system replaces modes

### Classical (c. 1750-1820)

- **Characteristics:** Clarity, balance, symmetry, homophonic texture, dynamic contrast
- **Forms:** Sonata, symphony, string quartet, concerto, opera
- **Key figures:** Haydn, Mozart, Beethoven (transitional), Gluck
- Sonata form as the defining structural achievement

### Romantic (c. 1820-1900)

- **Characteristics:** Emotional expression, expanded orchestra, chromaticism, nationalism, program music
- **Key figures:** Chopin, Liszt, Wagner, Brahms, Tchaikovsky, Dvorak, Mahler, Verdi
- **Developments:** Leitmotif (Wagner), tone poem (Liszt/Strauss), art song (Schubert, Schumann)
- Harmony stretches toward atonality

### 20th Century and Beyond

- **Impressionism:** Debussy, Ravel — whole-tone, modal, parallel chords, timbre as element
- **Expressionism/Atonality:** Schoenberg, Berg, Webern — free atonality → twelve-tone serialism
- **Neoclassicism:** Stravinsky, Prokofiev — return to classical forms with modern harmony
- **Minimalism:** Reich, Glass, Riley, Adams — repetition, gradual process, tonal
- **Aleatoric/Chance:** Cage — indeterminacy, prepared piano, 4'33"
- **Spectral music, electronic music, musique concrète, computer music**

## X. Jazz Harmony

### ii-V-I Progression

The fundamental jazz cadence:
- In C major: Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7
- In C minor: Dm7b5 → G7(b9) → Cm(maj7)

### Chord Extensions and Alterations

Jazz routinely uses 9ths, 11ths, 13ths, and altered tones:
- Cmaj9, Dm11, G13, G7(#9b13), G7alt
- **Upper structure triads:** Major triad superimposed over dominant (e.g., Eb/G7 = G7#5b9)

### Tritone Substitution

Replace V7 with bII7 (shares the same tritone):
- G7 → Db7 → Cmaj7 (chromatic bass descent: Db → C)

### Modal Jazz

- **Miles Davis, *Kind of Blue* (1959):** Solos based on modes rather than chord changes
- D Dorian, Eb Dorian, Bb Lydian — each section stays on one chord/mode

### Coltrane Changes (Giant Steps)

- Cycle of major thirds: B → G → Eb → B
- Three tonal centers equally dividing the octave
- ii-V-I in each key: rapid modulation

### Rhythm Changes

Based on Gershwin's "I Got Rhythm":
- A sections: I-vi-ii-V turnarounds
- Bridge: III7-VI7-II7-V7 (cycle of dominants)

## XI. Latin American Music

### Son (Cuba)

- Foundation of salsa; emerged in eastern Cuba late 19th century
- **Clave:** Rhythmic pattern organizing the music (son clave: 3-2 or 2-3)
  - 3-2 son clave: X..X..X. | ..X.X... (in 4/4)
- **Montuno:** Repeated piano vamp, call-and-response vocal section
- **Instruments:** Tres, maracas, bongos, bass, trumpet, claves

### Bossa Nova (Brazil)

- Fusion of samba rhythm with jazz harmony (late 1950s)
- **Key figures:** João Gilberto, Antonio Carlos Jobim, Vinícius de Moraes
- **Harmonic sophistication:** Extended chords, chromatic movement, ii-V chains
- **Rhythm:** Syncopated guitar pattern; understated, intimate
- *The Girl from Ipanema*, *Corcovado*, *Desafinado*

### Tango (Argentina/Uruguay)

- Emerged in Buenos Aires port neighborhoods (late 19th century)
- **Bandoneón** as signature instrument (+ violin, piano, double bass)
- **Harmonic language:** Minor keys, diminished chords, chromaticism
- **Forms:** Milonga (faster precursor), tango-canción, vals criollo
- **Astor Piazzolla:** Nuevo tango — fusion with jazz and classical; *Libertango*, *Adiós Nonino*

### Cumbia (Colombia)

- Indigenous, African, and Spanish roots; from Caribbean coast of Colombia
- **Rhythm:** Steady, shuffling beat; typically in 4/4 or 2/4
- **Instruments:** Gaitas (flutes), tambores (drums), guacharaca, accordion (later)
- Spread across Latin America; regional variants (Mexican cumbia, Peruvian chicha, Argentine cumbia villera)

### Norteño / Conjunto (Mexico/Texas)

- Accordion-driven music of northern Mexico and South Texas
- **Instruments:** Accordion, bajo sexto (12-string bass guitar), bass, drums
- **Forms:** Polka, ranchera, corrido (narrative ballad), bolero ranchero
- **Corrido:** Story-song tradition; narcocorrido as controversial modern variant

### Mariachi (Mexico)

- Originated in Jalisco; national symbol of Mexican music
- **Instruments:** Violins, trumpets, guitarrón (bass), vihuela, guitar
- **Repertoire:** Son jalisciense, ranchera, bolero, huapango
- Vocal style: powerful, ornamented, often with grito (shout)

### Other Traditions

- **Salsa:** Urbanized son + jazz influences (NYC, 1960s-70s) — Fania All-Stars, Héctor Lavoe, Celia Cruz
- **Merengue:** Dominican Republic; fast 2/4, accordion/saxophone
- **Vallenato:** Colombian; accordion, caja, guacharaca
- **Andean music:** Quena, charango, zampoña — pentatonic melodies
- **Reggaeton/Dembow:** Urban Latin; syncopated "dembow" riddim
