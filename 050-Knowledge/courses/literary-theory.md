# Literary Theory & Latin American Literature

> [[../literature-syllabus|Literature & Language Syllabus]] | Related: [[linguistics]], [[music-theory]]

## Motivation

Literary theory provides the critical lenses through which we read, interpret, and argue about texts. Far from being an abstract academic exercise, these frameworks shape how we understand culture, power, identity, and meaning — not just in literature but in film, media, politics, and everyday discourse. This course surveys the major schools of thought from formalism through ecocriticism, then turns to Latin American literature, one of the richest and most innovative traditions in world letters, where the interplay of colonial history, indigenous cultures, political turmoil, and radical aesthetic experimentation produced a literary explosion that reshaped the global literary landscape.

## Prerequisites

- No formal prerequisites
- Familiarity with major literary works helps but is not required
- Reading knowledge of Spanish is beneficial for the Latin American section but not essential

## I. Formalism and New Criticism

### Russian Formalism (1910s-1930s)

- **Key figures:** Shklovsky, Jakobson, Eichenbaum, Tynyanov
- **Literariness (literaturnost):** What makes a text literary vs. ordinary language?
- **Defamiliarization (ostranenie):** Art makes the familiar strange, renewing perception
- **Fabula vs. sjuzhet:** Story (chronological events) vs. plot (how events are arranged/narrated)
- Focus on **form and technique** rather than content, biography, or historical context

### Anglo-American New Criticism (1930s-1960s)

- **Key figures:** Brooks, Warren, Wimsatt, Beardsley, Ransom, Empson
- **The text as autonomous object:** "The poem itself" — close reading is primary
- **Intentional fallacy:** Meaning ≠ author's intention
- **Affective fallacy:** Meaning ≠ reader's emotional response
- **Paradox, irony, ambiguity, tension:** The hallmarks of literary language
- **Practical criticism:** Close reading of diction, imagery, structure, tone

## II. Structuralism

### Foundations: Ferdinand de Saussure

- **Sign = signifier (sound-image) + signified (concept)**
- **Arbitrariness of the sign:** No natural connection between word and meaning
- **Langue vs. parole:** Language system vs. individual speech acts
- **Synchronic vs. diachronic:** Study language as a system at a moment vs. through time
- **Difference:** Signs defined by what they are NOT (binary oppositions)

### Literary Structuralism

- **Roland Barthes (early):** Mythologies, narrative codes (S/Z: hermeneutic, proairetic, semic, symbolic, cultural)
- **Gérard Genette:** Narratology — focalization, narration levels, temporal order (analepsis/prolepsis), duration, frequency
- **Claude Lévi-Strauss:** Structural analysis of myth — binary oppositions as deep structure
- **Vladimir Propp:** 31 narrative functions in Russian fairy tales
- **Tzvetan Todorov:** Grammar of narrative; coined "narratology"
- **A.J. Greimas:** Actantial model (subject, object, sender, receiver, helper, opponent)

### Semiotics (Peirce, Eco)

- **Icon, index, symbol** as sign types
- **Unlimited semiosis:** Signs refer to other signs endlessly
- Interpretation is always mediated by codes and conventions

## III. Post-Structuralism

### Jacques Derrida and Deconstruction

- **Logocentrism:** Western metaphysics privileges presence, speech, and fixed meaning
- **Différance:** Meaning is endlessly deferred and constituted by difference (pun: to differ + to defer)
- **Binary oppositions:** Speech/writing, nature/culture, presence/absence — deconstruction shows the "subordinate" term is always already present in the "dominant" one
- **Supplementarity:** The supplement both adds to and substitutes for what it supplements
- **There is nothing outside the text (il n'y a pas de hors-texte):** All experience is textually mediated
- **Aporia:** Irresolvable contradiction within a text's logic

### Michel Foucault

- **Discourse:** Systems of knowledge/power that constitute what can be said, thought, or done
- **Episteme:** The unconscious structure of knowledge in a historical period
- **Power/knowledge:** Power is not just repressive but productive — it produces subjects, truths, institutions
- **The author function:** "What is an Author?" — the author as a discursive construct
- **Archaeology and genealogy** as methods of historical analysis
- **Panopticism and surveillance:** Architecture of power (from Discipline and Punish)
- **Biopolitics:** Power over life and populations

### Other Post-Structuralist Thinkers

- **Gilles Deleuze & Félix Guattari:** Rhizome (non-hierarchical), deterritorialization, schizoanalysis
- **Jean Baudrillard:** Simulacra and simulation; hyperreality
- **Jean-François Lyotard:** "Incredulity toward metanarratives" — the postmodern condition

## IV. Marxist Criticism

### Foundations

- **Base/superstructure model:** Economic relations (base) determine cultural/ideological forms (superstructure)
- **Ideology:** False consciousness that naturalizes class domination
- **Commodity fetishism:** Social relations between people appear as relations between things (Marx, Capital)

### Key Thinkers

- **Antonio Gramsci:** **Hegemony** — ruling class maintains power through cultural consent, not just force; counter-hegemonic struggle
- **Louis Althusser:**
  - **Ideological State Apparatuses (ISAs):** Church, school, media, family — reproduce ideology
  - **Interpellation:** Ideology "hails" individuals as subjects ("Hey, you!")
  - **Symptomatic reading:** What a text cannot say reveals ideology's contradictions
- **Fredric Jameson:**
  - *The Political Unconscious*: "Always historicize!" — every text is a symbolic resolution of real social contradictions
  - **Postmodernism as the cultural logic of late capitalism:** Pastiche, depthlessness, waning of affect
  - Three horizons of interpretation: text, social, mode of production
- **Terry Eagleton:** Accessible Marxist criticism; *Literary Theory: An Introduction*
- **Raymond Williams:** Culture as "structures of feeling"; cultural materialism
- **Walter Benjamin:** "The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction" — aura, authenticity, political potential

## V. Feminist Theory and Gender Studies

### Waves and Key Figures

- **Simone de Beauvoir:** *The Second Sex* (1949) — "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman." Sex vs. gender; woman as Other
- **Kate Millett:** *Sexual Politics* — literature as a site of patriarchal ideology
- **Elaine Showalter:** Gynocriticism — studying women's writing on its own terms; three phases: feminine, feminist, female
- **Sandra Gilbert & Susan Gubar:** *The Madwoman in the Attic* — 19th-century women writers negotiating patriarchal literary forms

### Écriture Féminine

- **Hélène Cixous:** "The Laugh of the Medusa" — women must write themselves into existence; writing the body
- **Luce Irigaray:** Critique of phallogocentrism; feminine language as fluid, plural
- **Julia Kristeva:** Semiotic (pre-linguistic, maternal, rhythmic) vs. symbolic (paternal, linguistic, law)

### Judith Butler and Gender Performativity

- *Gender Trouble* (1990): Gender is not an essence but a performance — constituted by repeated acts
- **Performativity:** Gender is what you do, not what you are
- **Drag and subversion:** Parody reveals the constructed nature of "original" gender
- Destabilizes the sex/gender distinction itself

### Intersectionality (Crenshaw)

- Race, class, gender, sexuality as interlocking systems of oppression — cannot be analyzed separately

## VI. Psychoanalytic Criticism

### Sigmund Freud

- **The unconscious:** Repressed desires, memories, and conflicts manifest in dreams, slips, and art
- **Dream-work:** Condensation, displacement, symbolization, secondary revision
- **Oedipus complex:** Foundational drama of desire, identification, and prohibition
- **Id, ego, superego:** Structural model of the psyche
- **The uncanny (das Unheimliche):** The familiar made strange — source of aesthetic horror
- **Literature as sublimation:** Artistic creation channels unconscious desires

### Jacques Lacan

- **The Imaginary, Symbolic, Real:** Three orders structuring subjectivity
- **Mirror stage:** Infant identifies with a unified image (misrecognition) → the ego as fiction
- **The Symbolic:** Language, law, social order; the Name-of-the-Father
- **The Real:** What escapes symbolization; the traumatic remainder
- **Desire:** Always desire of the Other; structured by lack; metonymic chain
- **Signifier over signified:** Meaning slides; the unconscious is structured like a language
- **Jouissance:** Pleasure-in-pain beyond the pleasure principle
- **The objet petit a:** Object-cause of desire; never attainable

## VII. Postcolonial Theory

### Edward Said — *Orientalism* (1978)

- The "Orient" as a Western construct: discourse produces knowledge that serves imperial power
- Orientalism as a system of representations, stereotypes, and power relations
- Link between knowledge production and colonial domination

### Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak

- "Can the Subaltern Speak?" (1988): Subaltern subjects (especially women in the Global South) are structurally silenced by both colonial and nationalist discourses
- **Strategic essentialism:** Temporarily adopting a unified identity for political mobilization
- Deconstruction + Marxism + feminism in postcolonial analysis

### Homi K. Bhabha

- **Hybridity:** Colonial identities are mixed, unstable, never fully colonial or indigenous
- **Mimicry:** "Almost the same, but not quite" — colonial subject imitates colonizer, producing uncanny disturbance
- **Third space:** In-between space where new cultural meanings emerge
- **Ambivalence:** Colonial discourse is simultaneously attracted to and repelled by the Other

### Frantz Fanon

- *Black Skin, White Masks*: Psychology of colonization; the colonized internalizes the colonizer's gaze
- *The Wretched of the Earth*: Violence as necessary for decolonization; national consciousness
- Foundational for all subsequent postcolonial thought

---

## VIII. Other Critical Schools

### Reader-Response Theory

- **Wolfgang Iser:** Implied reader; gaps and blanks that the reader fills
- **Stanley Fish:** Interpretive communities determine meaning; "Is There a Text in This Class?"
- **Hans Robert Jauss:** Horizon of expectations; reception history

### New Historicism (Greenblatt)

- Literature and history are mutually constitutive; no "background" — everything is text
- **Cultural poetics:** How texts circulate social energy
- Anecdotal method: juxtapose literary and non-literary texts from the same period
- **Cultural materialism (British counterpart):** Williams, Dollimore, Sinfield — more explicitly political

### Ecocriticism

- Literature and the environment; nature as more than backdrop
- **First wave:** Wilderness writing, nature poetry, environmental advocacy
- **Second wave:** Environmental justice, urban environments, postcolonial ecocriticism
- **Material ecocriticism:** Agency of non-human matter
- Key texts: Rachel Carson's *Silent Spring*, Thoreau's *Walden*

## IX. Latin American Literature

### Historical Context

- Colonial literature (chronicles, Sor Juana Inés de la Cruz)
- 19th century: Romanticism, costumbrismo, modernismo (Rubén Darío)
- **Vanguardia:** Latin American avant-garde (1920s-40s): Huidobro, Vallejo, Neruda
- **The Boom (1960s-70s):** International explosion of Latin American fiction
- **Post-Boom:** Return to realism, testimonial, women's writing

### Magical Realism (Lo Real Maravilloso)

- **Alejo Carpentier:** Coined "lo real maravilloso" — the marvelous is inherent in Latin American reality (not imported fantasy)
- **Characteristics:** Matter-of-fact narration of extraordinary events; blending of myth and history; cyclical time; collective memory
- Distinguished from European fantasy/surrealism: rooted in lived cultural experience, not aesthetic play
- Not all Latin American literature is magical realism — this is a reductive stereotype

### Jorge Luis Borges (1899-1986)

- Argentine; the most influential Latin American writer of the 20th century
- **Key works:** *Ficciones*, *El Aleph*, *El Jardín de Senderos que se Bifurcan*
- **Themes:**
  - **Labyrinths:** Physical and metaphysical; reality as labyrinthine
  - **Infinity:** The infinite library (Library of Babel), the Aleph (point containing all points)
  - **Time:** Circular, branching, simultaneous ("The Garden of Forking Paths")
  - **Mirrors and doubles:** Multiplication of identity
  - **The book and the world:** Reality as text; texts containing/generating reality
- **Style:** Erudite, concise, blurs fiction and essay; invents fictional authors, books, encyclopedias
- Preceded and influenced the Boom; connected Latin American lit to world philosophical traditions

### Gabriel García Márquez (1927-2014)

- Colombian; Nobel Prize 1982
- ***Cien Años de Soledad* (1967):** Foundational novel of magical realism
  - **Macondo:** Fictional town as microcosm of Latin American history
  - Circular time; history as repetition; the Buendía family's doom
  - Political violence (banana massacre), solitude, incest, decay
  - "Many years later, as he faced the firing squad..." — the novel's legendary opening
- ***El Amor en los Tiempos del Cólera, Crónica de una Muerte Anunciada, El Otoño del Patriarca***
- Journalism and literature inseparable; testimonial impulse

### Juan Rulfo (1917-1986)

- Mexican; two short works that changed everything
- ***Pedro Páramo* (1955):**
  - Juan Preciado travels to Comala to find his father → discovers a ghost town
  - Boundaries between living and dead, past and present, dissolve
  - Fragmented, non-linear narrative; voices of the dead narrate
  - Precursor to magical realism; profoundly influenced García Márquez
  - Landscape of death, caciquismo (political bossism), the Mexican Revolution's aftermath
- ***El Llano en Llamas* (1953):** Short stories of rural Mexico; sparse, devastating prose

### Octavio Paz (1914-1998)

- Mexican poet, essayist, diplomat; Nobel Prize 1990
- ***El Laberinto de la Soledad* (1950):**
  - Essay on Mexican identity: the mask, solitude, fiesta, La Malinche, the Conquest
  - **La Chingada:** The violated mother as foundational myth
  - Mexicans as "hijos de la Chingada" — children of violation, caught between indigenous and Spanish identities
  - Solitude as the defining condition of Mexican (and universal) existence
- **Poetry:** *Piedra de Sol*, *Blanco* — surrealist influence, eroticism, Eastern philosophy
- **The Bow and the Lyre:** Theory of poetry

### Julio Cortázar (1914-1984)

- Argentine (lived in Paris); master of the fantastic short story
- ***Rayuela* (Hopscotch, 1963):**
  - Anti-novel: two ways to read (linear chapters 1-56 or "hopscotch" order including expendable chapters)
  - Horacio Oliveira in Paris and Buenos Aires; search for authenticity
  - Challenges conventions of reading, narrative, and the novel form
  - Jazz as structural principle; language as both prison and liberation
- **Short stories:** "Casa Tomada," "Axolotl," "La Noche Boca Arriba," "Continuidad de los Parques"
- Influence of surrealism, jazz, and pataphysics

### Carlos Fuentes (1928-2012)

- Mexican; prolific novelist, essayist, diplomat
- ***La Muerte de Artemio Cruz* (1962):** Dying Mexican power broker's life in three narrative voices (yo/tú/él — I/you/he); the betrayed Mexican Revolution
- ***Terra Nostra* (1975):** Encyclopedic novel spanning Habsburg Spain and Mexican history
- ***La Región Más Transparente* (1958):** Modernist panorama of Mexico City
- Mestizo identity, failed revolutions, the persistence of the past

### Mario Vargas Llosa (1936-)

- Peruvian; Nobel Prize 2010
- ***La Ciudad y los Perros* (1963):** Military academy as microcosm of Peruvian society; structural complexity
- ***Conversación en La Catedral* (1969):** "At what precise moment had Peru fucked itself up?" — dictatorship, corruption, moral decay
- ***La Guerra del Fin del Mundo* (1981):** Epic of the Canudos rebellion in Brazil
- **Technique:** Telescoping dialogues, multiple timelines, "communicating vessels" (juxtaposed scenes illuminate each other)
- Political evolution: from leftist to liberal (controversial)

### Other Essential Figures

- **Roberto Bolaño:** *Los Detectives Salvajes*, *2666* — violence, literature, the abyss
- **Clarice Lispector:** Brazilian; *A Hora da Estrela*, *A Paixão Segundo G.H.* — interior consciousness
- **José Donoso:** Chilean; *El Obsceno Pájaro de la Noche* — grotesque, transformation
- **Elena Poniatowska:** Mexican; *La Noche de Tlatelolco* — testimonial literature
- **Rosario Castellanos, Diamela Eltit, Cristina Peri Rossi:** Women's voices in/after the Boom

## X. Theoretical Intersections with Latin American Literature

- **Postcolonial theory** and Latin American coloniality (Quijano: "coloniality of power")
- **Transculturation (Ortiz, Rama):** How cultures blend under unequal power relations
- **Testimonio:** First-person accounts of political violence and resistance (Rigoberta Menchú, controversial)
- **Dependency theory** and literary criticism
- **Neobaroque (Sarduy, Lezama Lima):** Excess, ornamentation, and play as resistance to colonial/imperial order
- **Subaltern studies** in Latin American context (Latin American Subaltern Studies Group)
