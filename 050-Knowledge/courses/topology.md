## 1. Motivation

Topology is the study of properties preserved under continuous deformations — stretching, bending, and twisting, but not tearing or gluing. It answers questions like: Is a space connected? Does it have holes? Can a map be continuously deformed into another? These qualitative, structural questions arise everywhere in mathematics, physics, data science, and computer science.

Point-set topology provides the foundational framework (what does "continuous" even mean in general?). Algebraic topology assigns algebraic invariants (groups, rings) to spaces, allowing us to distinguish spaces that "look different" in a rigorous sense. Differential topology studies smooth manifolds using tools from calculus and connects tightly to [[differential-geometry]].

## 2. Prerequisites

- **Real analysis:** Metric spaces, open/closed sets, continuity, compactness in R^n.
- **Linear algebra:** Vector spaces, linear maps.
- **Abstract algebra basics:** Groups, homomorphisms, quotient groups, rings (for cohomology).

## 3. Detailed Topic Outline

### Part I: Point-Set Topology

#### 3.1 Topological Spaces and Continuous Maps

- **Definition.** A topology on X is a collection τ of subsets (called open) closed under arbitrary unions and finite intersections, containing ∅ and X.
- **Intuition:** A topology tells you which points are "near" which other points, without reference to distance.
- Continuous maps: f: X → Y is continuous iff f^{-1}(U) is open for every open U ⊂ Y.
- Homeomorphisms: continuous bijections with continuous inverse. The "isomorphisms" of topology.
- Bases and subbases for a topology.
- Examples: discrete, indiscrete, cofinite, order topology, metric topology, product topology, quotient topology.

#### 3.2 Constructions

- **Subspace topology:** Induced by intersection.
- **Product topology:** Generated by products of open sets in finitely many factors. (Not the box topology!)
- **Quotient topology:** Identifying points; the quotient map is a surjection making the topology as fine as possible.
- Examples: torus T^2 = R^2/Z^2, Mobius band, Klein bottle, projective space RP^n = S^n/~.

#### 3.3 Connectedness

- **Connected:** Cannot be written as a disjoint union of two nonempty open sets.
- **Path-connected:** Any two points joined by a continuous path. Path-connected implies connected; converse fails in general (topologist's sine curve).
- Connected components; path components.
- **Intermediate Value Theorem** is a corollary: continuous image of a connected space is connected.

#### 3.4 Compactness

- **Definition.** Every open cover has a finite subcover.
- **Intuition:** Compact spaces behave like finite spaces in many ways — continuous functions achieve their bounds, limits exist, and many constructions "work."
- Compact subsets of R^n: Heine-Borel theorem (compact ⟺ closed and bounded).
- Key properties: continuous image of compact is compact; closed subset of compact is compact; compact subset of Hausdorff is closed.
- **Tychonoff's theorem:** Arbitrary product of compact spaces is compact (in the product topology). Equivalent to the axiom of choice.
- Sequential compactness vs. compactness: equivalent in metric spaces, not in general.
- Local compactness; one-point compactification (Alexandroff). Example: S^n = R^n ∪ {∞}.
- Compactness in [[functional-analysis]]: Banach-Alaoglu theorem.

#### 3.5 Separation Axioms

- **T0 (Kolmogorov):** Points are topologically distinguishable.
- **T1:** Points are closed.
- **T2 (Hausdorff):** Distinct points have disjoint neighborhoods. The "standard" assumption.
- **T3 (Regular):** Points and closed sets can be separated by open sets (plus T1).
- **T4 (Normal):** Disjoint closed sets can be separated by open sets (plus T1).
- **Urysohn's Lemma:** In a normal space, disjoint closed sets can be separated by a continuous function [0,1]-valued.
- **Tietze Extension Theorem:** Continuous functions on closed subsets of normal spaces extend to the whole space.

#### 3.6 Metrization

- **Urysohn Metrization Theorem:** A second-countable regular space is metrizable.
- **Nagata-Smirnov Metrization Theorem:** A space is metrizable iff it is regular and has a σ-locally finite base.
- Completely metrizable spaces (Polish spaces) — important in descriptive set theory and probability.

#### 3.7 Countability and Paracompactness

- First and second countable spaces. Separable spaces.
- **Paracompactness:** Every open cover has a locally finite refinement. Metrizable spaces are paracompact.
- Partitions of unity (connection to [[differential-geometry]]).

### Part II: Algebraic Topology

#### 3.8 The Fundamental Group

- **Homotopy** of maps: f ≃ g means there is a continuous deformation F: X × [0,1] → Y with F(·,0) = f, F(·,1) = g.
- **Homotopy equivalence:** X ≃ Y if there exist f: X → Y, g: Y → X with gf ≃ id_X, fg ≃ id_Y. Weaker than homeomorphism.
- **Loops** based at x_0: continuous maps γ: [0,1] → X with γ(0) = γ(1) = x_0.
- **Fundamental group** π_1(X, x_0): homotopy classes of loops with concatenation.
- **Intuition:** π_1 detects "1-dimensional holes." If every loop can be contracted to a point, π_1 = 0 (simply connected).
- Computations:
  - π_1(R^n) = 0; π_1(S^1) ≅ Z; π_1(T^2) ≅ Z^2; π_1(S^n) = 0 for n ≥ 2.
  - π_1(figure-eight) ≅ F_2 (free group on 2 generators).
  - π_1(RP^n) ≅ Z/2 for n ≥ 2.
- **Van Kampen's theorem:** Computes π_1 of a union X = U ∪ V from π_1(U), π_1(V), π_1(U ∩ V).
- Functoriality: continuous maps induce group homomorphisms on π_1.

#### 3.9 Covering Spaces

- **Covering space:** A map p: X̃ → X where every point has an evenly covered neighborhood.
- Examples: R → S^1 (universal cover), S^n → RP^n (double cover).
- **Path lifting** and **homotopy lifting** properties.
- **Classification theorem:** For a path-connected, locally path-connected, semi-locally simply connected space X, covering spaces of X (up to isomorphism) correspond bijectively to conjugacy classes of subgroups of π_1(X).
- **Universal cover:** The simply connected covering space. Its deck transformation group is π_1(X).
- **Intuition:** Covering spaces "unwind" the loops of X, and the fundamental group organizes this unwinding.

#### 3.10 Singular Homology

- **Singular simplices:** Continuous maps σ: Δ^n → X from the standard n-simplex.
- **Chain groups** C_n(X) = free abelian group on singular n-simplices.
- **Boundary map** ∂_n: C_n → C_{n-1}. Key property: ∂^2 = 0.
- **Homology groups** H_n(X) = ker ∂_n / im ∂_{n+1} = (cycles)/(boundaries).
- **Intuition:** H_n detects n-dimensional holes. H_0 counts connected components. H_1 ≅ π_1^{ab} (abelianized fundamental group). H_2 detects "cavities."
- Computations:
  - H_*(S^n) ≅ Z in degrees 0 and n, zero otherwise.
  - H_*(T^2) ≅ Z, Z^2, Z in degrees 0, 1, 2.
- **Functoriality:** Continuous maps induce homomorphisms on homology. Homotopic maps induce the same homomorphism.
- **Key tools:**
  - **Mayer-Vietoris sequence:** The long exact sequence relating H_*(U), H_*(V), H_*(U ∩ V), H_*(U ∪ V). The main computational tool.
  - **Excision:** H_*(X, A) ≅ H_*(X \ U, A \ U) when the closure of U is in the interior of A.
  - **Long exact sequence of a pair:** ... → H_n(A) → H_n(X) → H_n(X, A) → H_{n-1}(A) → ...
- **Euler characteristic** χ(X) = Σ (-1)^n rank H_n(X). A topological invariant.

#### 3.11 Cohomology

- **Singular cohomology** H^n(X; R) = Hom(H_n(X), R) (roughly; plus Ext terms via universal coefficient theorem).
- **Cup product:** H^p ⊗ H^q → H^{p+q} makes H*(X; R) a graded ring.
- **Intuition:** Cohomology carries a ring structure that homology lacks, giving strictly more information. The cup product detects "linking" phenomena.
- **de Rham cohomology** (for smooth manifolds): agrees with singular cohomology over R. See [[differential-geometry]].
- **Poincare duality:** For a closed oriented n-manifold, H^k ≅ H_{n-k}. Geometry meets algebra.

#### 3.12 CW Complexes

- Built inductively: start with points (0-cells), attach disks via boundary maps.
- **Cellular homology:** Much more computable than singular homology. The chain complex comes from the attaching maps.
- Every manifold and every "reasonable" space (simplicial complex, algebraic variety) has a CW structure.
- **Whitehead's theorem:** A map between CW complexes inducing isomorphisms on all homotopy groups is a homotopy equivalence.

#### 3.13 Higher Homotopy Groups

- **π_n(X, x_0):** Homotopy classes of maps (S^n, *) → (X, x_0).
- π_n is abelian for n ≥ 2.
- **Intuition:** π_n detects "n-dimensional holes" in a homotopy-theoretic sense, finer than homology.
- **Long exact sequence of a fibration:** ... → π_n(F) → π_n(E) → π_n(B) → π_{n-1}(F) → ...
- Homotopy groups of spheres: a central open problem. π_3(S^2) ≅ Z (Hopf fibration). The stable homotopy groups are finite for n > 0.
- **Hurewicz theorem:** The first nontrivial homotopy group equals the first nontrivial homology group (under connectivity assumptions).
- **Freudenthal suspension theorem:** Homotopy groups stabilize.

### Part III: Differential Topology

#### 3.14 Smooth Manifolds (Differential Topology Perspective)

- Smooth structures; exotic smooth structures (exotic R^4, Milnor's exotic 7-spheres).
- Sard's theorem: the set of critical values of a smooth map has measure zero.
- Whitney embedding theorem: every smooth n-manifold embeds in R^{2n+1}.

#### 3.15 Transversality

- **Transversality** of maps and submanifolds: f ⋔ W means df(T_pM) + T_{f(p)}W = T_{f(p)}N at every point of f^{-1}(W).
- **Transversality theorem:** Transversality is generic — any map can be perturbed to be transverse.
- **Applications:** Intersection theory, degree of a map.

#### 3.16 Morse Theory

- **Morse functions:** Smooth functions f: M → R whose critical points are non-degenerate (Hessian is nonsingular).
- **Morse lemma:** Near a critical point of index k, f looks like -x_1^2 - ... - x_k^2 + x_{k+1}^2 + ... + x_n^2.
- **Handle decomposition:** Passing through a critical point of index k attaches a k-handle (D^k × D^{n-k}).
- **Morse inequalities:** The number of critical points of index k bounds the Betti number b_k from above.
  - *Intuition:* The topology of M constrains the complexity of any smooth function on it.
- **Strong Morse inequalities** and the Morse polynomial.
- **Applications:** Proving the h-cobordism theorem, Bott periodicity, topology of loop spaces.

#### 3.17 Cobordism (Overview)

- Two closed n-manifolds M, N are cobordant if there exists an (n+1)-manifold W with ∂W = M ⊔ N.
- Cobordism groups Ω_n. Thom's theorem: Ω_n ≅ π_{n+k}(Th(γ_k)) — cobordism reduces to homotopy theory.
- Oriented cobordism, spin cobordism — important in topological quantum field theory.

## 4. Key Theorems — Summary

| Theorem | Domain | Significance |
|---------|--------|--------------|
| Tychonoff | Point-set | Products of compact spaces are compact |
| Urysohn's Lemma | Point-set | Separating closed sets by functions |
| Van Kampen | Algebraic | Computing fundamental groups of unions |
| Mayer-Vietoris | Algebraic | Computing homology of unions |
| Poincare Duality | Algebraic | H^k ≅ H_{n-k} for closed oriented manifolds |
| Hurewicz | Algebraic | First nontrivial π_n = first nontrivial H_n |
| Whitehead | Algebraic | π_* isomorphisms ⟹ homotopy equivalence (CW) |
| Sard | Differential | Critical values have measure zero |
| Transversality | Differential | Generic maps are transverse |
| Morse Inequalities | Differential | Critical points bound Betti numbers |

## 5. Applications

- **Physics:** Topological quantum field theory, topological insulators, gauge theory (connections to [[differential-geometry]]), anyons in 2D materials.
- **Data science:** Persistent homology (topological data analysis / TDA) — extracting topological features from point clouds.
- **Computer science:** Distributed computing impossibility results (using simplicial complexes), topological methods in combinatorics (Borsuk-Ulam theorem).
- **Analysis:** Fixed point theorems (Brouwer, Lefschetz), degree theory for nonlinear PDEs. See [[functional-analysis]].
- **Number theory:** Etale cohomology, topological methods in algebraic geometry. See [[number-theory]].
- **Robotics:** Configuration spaces, motion planning complexity (topological complexity of Farber).

## 7. Exercises and Milestones

- [ ] Prove that [0,1] is compact directly from the definition (finite subcover argument).
- [ ] Show that a continuous bijection from a compact space to a Hausdorff space is a homeomorphism.
- [ ] Compute π_1(S^1) using covering space theory (R → S^1).
- [ ] Use Van Kampen to compute π_1 of the Klein bottle.
- [ ] Compute H_*(T^2) using Mayer-Vietoris.
- [ ] Compute the homology of RP^2 using its CW structure.
- [ ] Prove the Brouwer fixed point theorem using homology.
- [ ] Verify the Euler characteristic formula χ = V - E + F for a triangulation of S^2.
- [ ] Find a Morse function on the torus and identify its critical points and indices.
- [ ] Show that all closed oriented surfaces are cobordant to ∅ (they bound handlebodies).
