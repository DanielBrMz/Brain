# Medical Image Registration — Theory and Practice

> Rigorous treatment of the 3D brain registration problem as encountered in the FetEnhNet label projection pipeline. Documents the fundamental challenges, why classical methods fail on fetal MRI, and the mathematical reasons SynthMorph succeeds.

---

## 1. Problem Statement

Given two 3D volumes of the same anatomical structure (e.g., a brain) acquired or reconstructed in different coordinate frames, find the spatial transformation `T` that aligns the moving volume `M` to the fixed volume `F`:

```
T* = argmin_T  D(F(x), M(T(x)))  for x ∈ domain(F)
```

Where `D` is some dissimilarity metric (mutual information, mean squared error, cross-correlation, etc.).

For our fetal MRI case, `T` is an **affine** transformation (12 DoF: 3 rotation + 3 translation + 3 scaling + 3 shear) or a **rigid** transformation (6 DoF: 3 rotation + 3 translation). Sometimes we need **deformable** transforms (stationary velocity fields, dense displacement fields).

---

## 2. The Coordinate System Zoo

The FetEnhNet pipeline passes data through multiple coordinate systems, each with a different affine-to-world matrix:

| Space | File | Resolution | World Frame |
|-------|------|------------|-------------|
| **Atlas space** | `recon_to31_nuc.nii`, `segmentation_to31_final.nii` | 0.5 mm iso | 31-week template canonical frame |
| **Pipeline recon** | `recon.nii` | 0.5 mm iso | Pipeline's internal frame (NOT scanner) |
| **NeSVoR default** | `recon.nii.gz` (without flag) | 0.8 mm iso | Atlas-aligned by SVoRT (scanner rotation zeroed) |
| **NeSVoR scanner-space** | `recon.nii.gz` (with `--scanner-space`) | 0.8 mm iso | True scanner world coords |
| **Raw scanner space** | `<session>/raw/*.nii` | 0.5-3 mm anisotropic | Native scanner frame per stack |

**Critical discovery**: Pipeline recon is NOT in scanner coordinates. Verified via checkerboard comparison — pipeline produces sagittal brain for coronal input. The pipeline's reconstruction algorithm creates its own learned frame.

**Key known transforms**:
- Atlas ↔ Pipeline: `recon_to31.xfm` (4x4 affine, generated by the pipeline)
- Raw ↔ Scanner NeSVoR: Direct `inv(A_nesvor_scanner) @ A_raw` (both live in scanner world)

**The missing link**: Atlas ↔ Scanner NeSVoR. No pre-computed transform exists.

## 3. Why This Registration Problem Is Hard

### 3.1 Large and Variable Initial Rotations

The fetal brain's orientation in the scanner depends on how the baby was positioned at scan time. Across subjects this is essentially random — the fetus can be head-up, head-down, left-facing, right-facing, and everything in between. The rotation between scanner coordinates and atlas coordinates can be anywhere in SO(3).

Classical gradient-based optimizers (gradient descent, LBFGS, conjugate gradient) have a **convergence radius** typically on the order of 20-45°. For rotations larger than this, they fall into local minima.

### 3.2 Shape Symmetry → Ambiguous Global Minima

The brain has approximate **sagittal mirror symmetry** (C2 symmetry). For any registration metric that depends only on anatomical content (not chirality-breaking features), there exist at least **two equivalent global minima** differing by a 180° rotation about the anterior-posterior axis.

Binary shape-based registration (matching brain masks) is the worst case — the mask has effectively more symmetry than the intensity volume, because all anatomical texture is discarded.

### 3.3 Non-Convexity of the Cost Landscape

Even for well-constrained metrics like Mattes Mutual Information, the cost landscape on brain volumes has:
- Many shallow local minima from partial anatomical overlaps
- Sharp basins around cortical landmarks
- Broad flat regions in CSF/background

The number of local minima grows exponentially with the dimensionality of `T` (12 for affine). Optimization-based methods can only guarantee convergence to a local minimum, not the global one.

### 3.4 Size, Resolution, and FOV Mismatch

| Volume | Dims | Resolution | FOV |
|--------|------|-----------|-----|
| Atlas | ~201×273×216 | 0.5 mm | ~100×137×108 mm |
| Scanner NeSVoR | ~143×123×114 | 0.8 mm | ~114×98×91 mm |
| Raw HASTE | variable | 0.5-1 mm in-plane, 3-5 mm slice | Full-torso or brain-focused |

Registration metrics on downsampled/cropped volumes can converge to spurious alignments because the moving volume may fit partially within the fixed FOV in multiple ways.

### 3.5 Subject-Specific Variability

- **Gestational age**: Brain size varies 2-3x across 24-36 weeks GA
- **Pathology**: CHD fetuses may have abnormal ventricle sizes, cortical delays
- **Acquisition quality**: Motion, SNR, field inhomogeneities differ per scan

This means any fixed-parameter registration strategy (e.g., shrink factors [8,4,2,1]) that works for one subject may fail on another.

## 4. Why Our Classical Approaches Failed

### 4.1 Attempts Log

| # | Approach | Dice (FCB033) | Dice (FCB014) | Failure Mode |
|---|----------|---------------|---------------|--------------|
| 1 | Direct A_raw @ inv(A_atlas) | — | — | Different world frames |
| 2 | XFM (recon_to31.xfm) applied to raw | 0.457 | — | Pipeline ≠ scanner coords |
| 3-6 | Mask-guided refinements (rigid, similarity, affine) | 0.66-0.90 | — | Wrong anatomical plane |
| 7-9 | SimpleITK MI with various inits (MOMENTS, GEOMETRY) | 0.59-0.89 | ≈0 | Local minima, orientation wrong |
| 10-13 | Pipeline recon as intermediate | 0.036-0.594 | — | Same local minima issue |
| **14** | **Binary brain shape + MOMENTS + MSE + Powell** | **0.90** | **0.0003** | **Non-convex lottery** |
| 15 | Binary shape + flip_k correction | 0.90 | — | Per-subject flip doesn't generalize |
| 16 | Per-subject 8-flip search | 0.90 | 0.012 | Flip alone can't recover position errors |
| 17 | Pipeline chain with recon_to31.xfm + MI | 0.002 | — | MI got trapped at local minimum |

### 4.2 The "Lottery" Phenomenon

The binary brain registration worked for FCB033 because:
1. The Powell optimizer's random step sequence happened to follow a path that descended into the correct basin
2. The resulting transform was off by only a k-axis flip (sagittal mirror ambiguity)
3. Manual visual testing identified and corrected the flip

For FCB014, FCB018, FCB040, etc., the same algorithm with the same initialization finds a **different local minimum**. These minima are:
- Still roughly inside the brain volume (brain_pct ~30% in NeSVoR space)
- But anatomically misaligned in 3D (seg lands in wrong part of skull)
- Not correctable by any of the 8 axis flips (flips only fix orientation, not position)

### 4.3 The Math of Why

For binary brain mask registration with MSE:
```
J(T) = ||M_fixed - M_moving(T(x))||²
```

The gradient is dominated by voxels near the brain boundary. With Powell optimization (coordinate-descent along rotated axes), the search direction at each step depends on the current iterate. Different starting rotations produce different descent trajectories, each converging to whatever local minimum is closest.

**This is not a bug in the algorithm** — it's the fundamental limit of derivative-free optimization on non-convex landscapes.

## 5. Mathematical Framework for Robust Registration

### 5.1 Decomposition

Robust registration needs two phases:
1. **Global search** (finds correct basin of attraction)
2. **Local refinement** (optimizes within the correct basin)

Our failures were all in phase 1. Phase 2 optimizers (gradient descent, Powell) are fine — they just need a good starting point.

### 5.2 Global Search Strategies

#### Multi-start

Sample N initializations uniformly on SO(3) (e.g., via Hopf fibration or random unit quaternions), run local refinement from each, pick best.

- **Pros**: Simple, deterministic convergence guarantee (for enough N)
- **Cons**: Expensive (O(N) registrations), N may need to be large (100-1000) for reliable results

#### Principal Axis Transform (PAT)

Compute 2nd-order moments (covariance matrix) of each shape. The eigenvectors give natural orthogonal axes. Align eigenvectors to get rotation.

- **Pros**: Deterministic, O(1) cost
- **Cons**: Axes of equal eigenvalues are undefined. For spherical-ish shapes (brain), this gives **noisy** axes. Doesn't solve chirality.

#### Landmark-based

Detect anatomical landmarks (e.g., AC-PC, brain stem, cerebellum) in each volume, solve Procrustes alignment.

- **Pros**: Uses chirality-breaking information
- **Cons**: Requires reliable landmark detection (a separate hard problem)

#### Fourier / Phase Correlation

Use spectral methods to find translations (via phase shift theorem). Combined with spherical harmonic decomposition, can also recover rotation.

- **Pros**: Fast, deterministic
- **Cons**: Requires well-sampled volumes, sensitive to noise/artifacts

### 5.3 Deep Learning Registration

Modern solution: **replace the iterative optimization entirely** with a learned function.

```
T = f_θ(M, F)
```

Where `f_θ` is a CNN trained on pairs of volumes and their ground-truth transforms.

Key innovations:
- **VoxelMorph** (Balakrishnan et al. 2018): Unsupervised training with smooth transformation regularization
- **SynthMorph** (Hoffmann et al. 2021): Trained on *synthetic* data with arbitrary rotations/translations → generalizes to any modality without tuning
- **KeyMorph** (Evan et al. 2022): Predicts keypoints for interpretable registration

**Why deep learning works here**:
1. Training distribution includes arbitrary rotations → network learns large convergence basin
2. Anatomy-aware features (learned from atlas) break chirality ambiguity
3. One forward pass = deterministic, no optimization to get stuck

**Limitations**:
- Requires training data or a pre-trained model
- May fail on OOD anatomy (e.g., severe pathology)
- Can't easily incorporate domain-specific priors

## 6. SynthMorph: Why It Works For Us

SynthMorph (available in FreeSurfer 7.4.1+ as `mri_synthmorph`) is a registration network trained on **synthetically generated** brain-like images:

1. **Training data synthesis**:
   - Sample random label maps from a generative model (SAMSEG-style brain parcellations)
   - Generate random intensity appearances (random contrast, noise, bias fields)
   - Generate random affine + deformable transforms
   - Task: predict the transform from the (synthetic_moving, synthetic_fixed) pair

2. **Why this generalizes**:
   - Network never sees real MRI — learns anatomy-agnostic features
   - Trained on arbitrary rotations → can handle up to 180° out of the box
   - Invariant to modality/contrast by construction

3. **Architecture**:
   - U-Net-like encoder + decoder
   - Predicts dense displacement field OR affine matrix depending on mode
   - Symmetric (registers A→B equivalently to B→A)

4. **For our problem**:
   - No training needed — use pre-trained weights
   - Single forward pass per subject
   - Handles arbitrary rotation/translation
   - Works on both T2 HASTE (raw) and the atlas template

5. **Command**:
   ```
   mri_synthmorph register -m affine -t <output.lta> <moving> <fixed>
   ```

## 7. Our Registration Pipeline

### 7.1 Fixed Infrastructure (Working)

```
Atlas space ↔ Pipeline recon: recon_to31.xfm (pipeline-generated)
Scanner NeSVoR ↔ Raw: inv(A_nesvor) @ A_raw (direct, since both in scanner world)
```

### 7.2 The New Link (SynthMorph)

```
Atlas recon → Scanner NeSVoR: mri_synthmorph register -m affine
```

### 7.3 Full Chain

```python
seg_atlas (in atlas space)
  ↓ [apply synthmorph LTA via mri_vol2vol --nearest]
seg_in_scanner (in scanner NeSVoR space)
  ↓ [direct voxel mapping with inv(A_nesvor) @ A_raw]
seg_on_raw (per-slice on each HASTE stack)
```

### 7.4 Validation

- Compute Dice between projected `seg_on_raw` and brain mask `mask_data` per stack
- Pick best stack (highest Dice) for gallery generation
- Target: Dice ≥ 0.85 across all subjects

## 8. Lessons for Future Medical Imaging Work

1. **Always verify coordinate frames with checkerboard tests** before assuming two volumes are co-aligned.
2. **Don't use binary shape registration** — it's the weakest form. Use intensity-based methods or learning-based methods.
3. **Classical optimizers are fine for refinement, not global search**. For cross-subject variability, use SynthMorph or multi-start.
4. **Trust but verify**: validate registration with an independent metric (e.g., brain mask Dice) on held-out data before scaling to a batch.
5. **FreeSurfer 8.1+ ships with SynthMorph built in** — no separate install needed. Run via Apptainer: `apptainer exec --nv <fs:8.1.0.sif> mri_synthmorph register`.
6. **The `.lta` transform format is FreeSurfer's native** — apply via `mri_vol2vol --lta <tx.lta> --mov <moving> --targ <fixed> --o <out> --nearest` (use `--nearest` for discrete labels like segmentations).

## 10. Why Registration Appears Simple But Isn't

A common intuition: "It's a 3D rotation and translation — just 12 numbers (or 6 rigid). Find 4 matching points and solve in closed form. Done."

**That intuition is exactly right — IF you have point correspondences.** Give 4 pairs of points you're certain correspond and you solve the affine in microseconds. But we don't have correspondences. We have two 3D arrays of scalar values. No line says "voxel (47, 83, 102) in atlas is the same anatomical location as voxel (31, 55, 68) in scanner." The correspondences must be *inferred from image content*.

### The inverse problem

- **Forward direction** (verify similarity given T): cheap — microseconds.
- **Inverse direction** (find T given images): *exponentially harder*.

Analogy: **verifying a password is trivial. Finding one is exponentially hard.** Registration is the same — verifying a good T is easy, finding one is not.

### Non-convexity in detail

The cost function `J(T) = -similarity(fixed, moving(T))` has many local minima:
- Each local minimum is a T where the images "look locally similar"
- Gradient descent walks to the *nearest* minimum, not the global one
- Basin of attraction around the true minimum is typically only ~30° wide

If the MOMENTS init lands within 30° of truth → gradient descent finds it.
If init is 90° off → descent falls into a *different* local minimum that's locally OK but globally wrong.

This explains the FetEnhNet data pattern: **FCB033 happened to init close enough, FCB014 didn't.** Lottery, not robustness.

### Symmetry as an information-theoretic barrier

The brain has approximate sagittal mirror symmetry. If T aligns atlas→scanner, then T composed with an L-R flip *also* "aligns" them with nearly equal similarity score. **This is not solvable from shape alone.** You need chirality-breaking information:
- Cerebellum is posterior-inferior
- Brain stem extends inferiorly
- (Minor) L/R cortical asymmetry

For fetal brains at 28-35 weeks GA, these features are subtle — fewer cortical folds, less developed asymmetry, NeSVoR reconstructions with different intensity profiles than the atlas.

### What we actually face

> Find the argmin of a 12-dimensional, non-convex, locally-smooth, chirality-ambiguous, modality-mismatched similarity function from an unknown starting point.

That's a genuinely hard problem.

### Why AI methods partially solve it

SynthMorph etc. don't optimize at test time — they've **learned** `(moving, fixed) → T` across millions of synthetic training pairs. They encode:
- What brains look like
- How rotations transform them
- Which local minima are spurious

Single forward pass, no optimization. But their generalization is limited by training distribution. Fetal MRI at atypical orientations is outside that distribution.

### The right decomposition: human + machine

- **Global search** (find correct basin): humans excel (millions of years of 3D spatial reasoning + anatomical priors). Optimizers are bad at this.
- **Local refinement** (converge within basin): optimizers excel. Humans are slow.

The purpose-built alignment GUI for FetEnhNet implements exactly this decomposition — human does 90° rotations to reach the right basin (seconds), optimizer refines (30s).
